How does conserved land benefit you?

When we conserve the land, we’re safeguarding fertile places to grow our food, clean air to breathe and water to drink, places for wildlife to make their homes as the climate changes, and natural places for us to explore. And, we’re also providing ourselves with the chance to live healthier lives —physically, mentally, and socially

Clean Water & Air:

Trees and plants are essential to the air we breathe and the water we drink. Forests absorb rainfall and snow melt, and help the soils filter ground water and recharge the aquifers and reservoirs that provide our drinking water.

Local Food:

Farmers must have productive land to grow the food we eat. Some of the best quality soils in the world are found in the Pioneer Valley. Since good farmland is often flat and open, farmlands are at risk of being lost to development unless they are permanently protected. Agriculture is also integral to our regional economy: farmland that has been conserved is less expensive for farmers to purchase, making farming more financially viable.

Places for Wildlife:

The Pioneer Valley is home to a rich diversity of wildlife from the largest black bear to the smallest dwarf mussel. Every species needs the right place and space to play its part in the web of life that sustains our region and our planet.

Climate Change:

The impacts of climate change are becoming clear, even in the Valley. Droughts, floods, fires, unpredictable weather events are a danger to our communities, local economies, and wildlife. Protecting large areas of forests can help slow the release of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. And, saving resilient landscapes may help wildlife and plants find new places to survive if their usual habitats change or disappear.


Land is a vital resource to humankind, like air and water. Land degradation—the deterioration or loss of the productive capacity of the soils for present and future—is a global challenge that affects everyone through food insecurity, higher food prices, climate change, environmental hazards, and the loss of biodiversity and ecosystem services. Land degradation is happening at an alarming pace, contributing to a dramatic decline in the productivity of croplands and rangelands worldwide. Land degradation is one of the world’s most pressing environmental problems and it will worsen without rapid remedial action. Globally, about 25 percent of the total land area has been degraded. When land is degraded, soil carbon and nitrous oxide is released into the atmosphere, making land degradation one of the most important contributors to climate change. Scientists recently warned that 24 billion tons of fertile soil was being lost per year, largely due to unsustainable agriculture practices. If this trend continues, 95 percent of the Earth’s land areas could become degraded by 2050. Globally, 3.2 billion people are affected by land degradation, especially rural communities, smallholder farmers, and the very poor. The world population is projected to increase by about 35 percent to 9.7 billion in 2050, with rising demands for agricultural products including food, feed, fiber, and fuel. However, pressure on the global land resource is increasing due to other factors as well, such as agricultural production systems made less resilient by the loss of biodiversity, and natural factors such as climate variability and extreme weather events. Climate change exacerbates variations in yields and income from agriculture, threatening the resilience of agro-ecosystems and stability of food production systems. The problems are particularly severe in the driest parts the planet. Dryland landscapes cover approximately 40 percent of the world’s land area and support two billion people. The vast majority of people who depend on drylands live in developing countries, where women and children are most vulnerable to the impacts of land degradation and drought.


Land resources are essential for various human activities, including agriculture, housing, infrastructure, and industrial development. Several factors affect land resources, both positively and negatively. Here are some of the key factors:

    • Population Growth: Rapid population growth increases the demand for land for housing, agriculture, and infrastructure development. This can lead to land scarcity and competition for available land resources.

      Urbanization: The expansion of urban areas and the development of cities can lead to the conversion of agricultural and natural lands into urban and industrial zones, reducing the availability of land for other purposes.

      Agricultural Practices: The choice of agricultural practices, such as intensive farming, mono-cropping, or agroforestry, can impact land resources and soil health. Unsustainable farming practices can lead to land degradation and loss of fertility.

      Land Use Planning: Effective land use planning and zoning regulations can influence how land is utilized. Proper planning can help protect valuable natural lands and ensure sustainable development.

      Degradation: Land degradation, including soil erosion, desertification, and salinization, can result from improper land management and overuse. It negatively affects land quality and productivity.

      Climate Change: Climate change can lead to shifts in temperature and precipitation patterns, impacting land resources. This can result in droughts, floods, and changes in ecosystems.

      Deforestation: The clearing of forests for agriculture, infrastructure, and other purposes can result in the loss of valuable land resources and biodiversity.
      Land Ownership and Tenure: Land tenure systems, property rights, and land distribution policies can affect how land is utilized and managed. Secure land tenure encourages investment in land improvement, while insecure land rights can lead to mismanagement.
      Pollution: Land pollution from industrial and agricultural runoff, hazardous waste, and other contaminants can degrade the quality of land resources and make them unsuitable for certain uses.

      Mining and Extraction: Extractive industries, such as mining and oil drilling, can have a significant impact on land resources, leading to habitat destruction and environmental contamination.


      Conservation Efforts: Efforts to protect and conserve land resources, such as the establishment of national parks and wildlife reserves, play a crucial role in preserving natural ecosystems.

      Land Reclamation: Some areas are subject to land reclamation projects to expand available land for development. However, this can have ecological and environmental consequences.

      Land Policy and Regulations: Government policies and regulations related to land ownership, land use, and land rights can significantly influence how land resources are managed and utilized.

      Technological Advancements : Technological advancements in agriculture, land management, and resource assessment can impact land use efficiency and productivity.

      Economic Factors : Economic conditions and market forces can influence the value and use of land resources. Real estate prices, commodity markets, and land speculation can all impact land resource decisions.
      Cultural and Social Factors: Cultural and social practices and beliefs can influence land use, such as indigenous land management practices and traditional agricultural methods.

    • It's essential to consider these factors when managing and planning the use of land resources to ensure sustainability, environmental protection, and the equitable distribution of land for various purposes.
